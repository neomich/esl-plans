The Magical Number 7

A2-B1 · General English · 90 min

Main Objectives

  • explore the cognitive principle of Miller’s Law (the “magical number 7 ± 2”) and how chunking enhances memory capacity;
  • practice using the Past Simple tense to narrate a fictional story (a movie plot) based on a video review;
  • apply critical thinking to real-world examples where the 7±2 limit shapes design, communication, and daily life.

About This Lesson

In this lesson, students begin with an engaging visual puzzle: a set of seemingly unrelated images (e.g., Continents, Wonders, Rainbow, Notes etc.), prompting them to search for a hidden pattern—leading naturally into the theme of cognitive grouping and mental limits. The core of the lesson is structured around three media tasks: Intro Audio + Game: Students listen to an audio clip that introduces the concept through a playful “game”. First Video (Concept Explainer): A short explainer video (featuring Dr. Raluca Budiu and referencing George Miller’s classic 1956 study) clearly defines: Chunks as meaningful units (not just letters or digits), The role of chunking in expanding memory capacity, Why “7 ± 2” is a robust empirical finding across domains (e.g., phone numbers, musical notes, team sizes). Students answer comprehension questions (e.g., Who studied human memory? What is the process called?) to verify their understanding. They then complete a matching task linking everyday examples (e.g., Meeting Agenda, Password Complexity, Dashboard Elements) to the idea of chunking — reinforcing that humans can reliably hold only ~5–9 discrete items in working memory. Second Video (Movie Review + Grammar Task): Students watch a fictional movie trailer/review for Lucky Numbers—a heist-style film about lottery fraud—and then complete a Past Simple gap-fill exercise reconstructing the plot. This reinforces regular/irregular verb forms while embedding grammar practice in a narrative context. This lesson elegantly bridges cognitive science, language form, and real-world relevance—making abstract psychology tangible through games, storytelling, and practical grammar use.
