How to Make Business English Lessons Actually Interesting

20 July 2026 · ESL-plans Teacher's Corner

Let's be honest. Business English doesn't have the best reputation.

Ask a teacher what comes to mind and you'll probably hear words like emails, meetings, negotiations, presentations... followed by a long sigh. The problem isn't Business English itself. The problem is that we've somehow convinced ourselves that business people only want to talk about... business.

After teaching professionals for years, I can confidently say that's simply not true. The engineer doesn't want to spend another hour discussing quarterly reports. The project manager has already survived six meetings today. The HR specialist definitely doesn't dream about role-playing another job interview. When they log into your lesson, they're looking for a break - not another shift at work. Here's what has worked for me:

Business English is still English

I rarely start a lesson by saying,

"Today we're going to practise negotiating."

Instead, I might ask:

"Would you rather have a four-day work week or a 20% pay rise?"

Or:

"Should companies monitor employees' computer activity?"

Or:

"Would AI make your job easier - or replace it?"

Now we're talking. Students forget they're in a Business English lesson because they're discussing something that actually affects them. The business language appears naturally.

Choose topics that people argue about

Nothing kills a lesson faster than a topic everyone agrees with.

"Good communication is important."

Well... yes. Conversation over. Instead, look for ideas that divide opinions.

  • Remote work.
  • Salary transparency.
  • Quiet quitting.
  • Corporate buzzwords.
  • Work-life balance.
  • AI in the workplace.
  • Office friendships.

These topics create genuine discussion because there isn't one correct answer.

Forget perfect role plays

I used to spend ages creating realistic business scenarios. Half the time they felt exactly like... work. Nowadays I keep role plays much more natural. Imagine you're asking your boss for a four-day work week. Convince your colleague to abandon an expensive project. Explain to your team why AI won't replace them. Students stop acting and start communicating. That's the difference.

Use authentic language

Business English textbooks often sound strangely polished. Real workplaces don't. People interrupt. They hesitate. They change direction halfway through a sentence. That's why I like using podcasts, interviews, TED Talks and real workplace discussions. Students need exposure to the kind of English they'll actually hear on Monday morning.

Let students bring their own experience

One of the biggest advantages of teaching adults is that they've already lived interesting professional lives.

Someone has managed a team. Someone has fired an employee. Someone has negotiated contracts. Someone has started a business.

Use that. Ask questions. Be curious. You'll often discover that your students provide better examples than any textbook ever could.

Don't ignore humour

Business doesn't have to mean serious. Some of my favourite conversations have started with ridiculous corporate jargon or bizarre office stories. People love sharing tales about pointless meetings, awkward interviews or impossible managers. Laughter breaks down barriers. It also gets people talking. And that's exactly what we want.

Borrow topics from outside business

Here's a trick I use all the time. Not every Business English lesson needs to be about business. A discussion about 12 Rules for Life naturally leads to conversations about future careers. A lesson on The Culture of Overwork fits perfectly with professionals. Even something like Why Do We Gossip? becomes surprisingly relevant once people start talking about office politics. Sometimes the best Business English lesson isn't labelled "Business English" at all.

Remember why they're there

Most professionals aren't learning English because they love grammar. They're learning because they want to communicate with colleagues, clients and partners without feeling anxious.

Help them do that:

  1. Encourage opinions.
  2. Interrupt less.
  3. Listen more.
  4. Build their confidence.

The vocabulary and grammar will come naturally when the conversation feels genuine. That's why I don't try to recreate another day at the office. I try to create the kind of conversation people wish they had during their coffee break. And somehow, that's where the best Business English lessons happen.
