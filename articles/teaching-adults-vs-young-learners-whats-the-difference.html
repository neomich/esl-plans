Teaching Adults vs Young Learners: What's the Difference?

22 July 2026 · ESL-plans Teacher's Corner

One of the questions I get asked surprisingly often is:

"Should I teach adults or children?"

My answer is always the same.

Neither is easier. They're just difficult in completely different ways.

I've spent most of my career teaching adults, so that's naturally where I feel at home. Give me an hour to discuss AI, philosophy or art, and I'm in my element. But I've also worked with younger learners, and they taught me something important:

The same lesson simply doesn't work for both audiences.

You can't just swap the worksheet and hope for the best. Adults and children learn differently, stay motivated differently and, perhaps most importantly, have completely different reasons for learning English. Here are the biggest differences I've noticed over the years.

Teaching Adults

1. Adults need a reason

Children are used to learning because... well, they're children. Adults are different. If you're asking them to learn ten new words, they'd quite like to know why. The more practical or personally interesting the lesson feels, the more engaged they'll become. That's one of the reasons I enjoy building lessons around psychology, technology and modern life instead of predictable textbook topics.

2. Adults bring experience

One of the greatest things about teaching adults is that they already have stories.

  • They've travelled.
  • Changed careers.
  • Raised families.
  • Made mistakes.
  • Started businesses.

Your job isn't to fill an empty cup. It's to create opportunities for them to talk about everything they already know. Sometimes the students become the most interesting part of the lesson.

3. Adults like conversations, not performances

Children usually enjoy games because they're fun. Adults enjoy games if they lead to an interesting discussion. I've found that the less my lesson feels like a classroom and the more it feels like two people chatting over coffee, the better it goes. That's why so many of my lesson plans revolve around authentic discussions rather than endless grammar exercises.

4. Adults are busy

Work. Family. Travel. Meetings. Life gets in the way. Sometimes they'll forget a lesson. Sometimes they'll join from a train or an airport. Flexibility is part of the job. A quick reminder, a follow-up message or a simple homework recap often goes much further than frustration ever will.

Teaching Young Learners

1. Energy beats explanation

Children don't want a five-minute explanation. They want to do something.

  • Move.
  • Guess.
  • Draw.
  • Compete.

The quicker they become active, the better.

2. Variety is everything

Young learners change gears quickly.

  • Reading.
  • Speaking.
  • A game.
  • A song.
  • A challenge.

Back to speaking. Keeping the pace lively often matters more than squeezing in one extra grammar point.

3. Visuals matter even more

Adults appreciate good visuals. Children practically depend on them. Bright images, interactive activities and colourful tasks help keep their attention focused while making new vocabulary much easier to remember.

4. Confidence comes before accuracy

Children are usually much happier taking risks with English. The goal isn't perfect grammar. It's building confidence and making English feel enjoyable. Accuracy comes later.

So... which is better?

Honestly? Neither. They're simply different.

Teaching adults often feels like hosting fascinating conversations with interesting people. Teaching children feels more like directing an energetic theatre performance. Both can be incredibly rewarding. You just need the right tools for the audience sitting in front of you.
