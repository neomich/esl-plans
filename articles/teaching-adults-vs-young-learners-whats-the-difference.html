Teaching Adults vs Young Learners: What's the Difference?

22 July 2026 · ESL-plans Teacher's Corner

Adults thrive on meaningful conversations and real-life topics, while young learners learn best through energy, variety and interactive activities. If you teach adults, you'll find plenty of discussion-based resources here at ESL Plans. If your focus is on children or teens, check out English Bright ESL and their growing collection of interactive ESL lessons.

One of the questions I get asked surprisingly often is: "Should I teach adults or children?"

My answer is always the same. Neither is easier. They're just difficult in completely different ways.

I've spent most of my career teaching adults. But I've also worked with younger learners, and they taught me something important: the same lesson simply doesn't work for both audiences.

Teaching Adults

1. Adults need a reason

Children are used to learning because they're children. Adults are different. The more practical or personally interesting the lesson feels, the more engaged they'll become.

2. Adults bring experience

One of the greatest things about teaching adults is that they already have stories. They've travelled. Changed careers. Raised families. Made mistakes. Started businesses. Your job isn't to fill an empty cup. It's to create opportunities for them to talk about everything they already know.

3. Adults like conversations, not performances

The less my lesson feels like a classroom and the more it feels like two people chatting over coffee, the better it goes.

4. Adults are busy

Work. Family. Travel. Meetings. Life gets in the way. Flexibility is part of the job.

Teaching Young Learners

1. Energy beats explanation

Children don't want a five-minute explanation. They want to move, guess, draw, compete. The quicker they become active, the better.

2. Variety is everything

Young learners change gears quickly. Keeping the pace lively often matters more than squeezing in one extra grammar point.

3. Visuals matter even more

Adults appreciate good visuals. Children practically depend on them.

4. Confidence comes before accuracy

The goal isn't perfect grammar. It's building confidence and making English feel enjoyable. Accuracy comes later.

Choosing the Right Resources

If your students are adults, that's exactly why I created ESL-plans.com. Most of my lessons are designed for B1-C1 learners and revolve around the kinds of conversations adults genuinely enjoy having.

It's an excellent resource for teachers looking to save preparation time while keeping younger learners motivated and actively involved in every lesson.

At the end of the day, great teaching isn't about choosing the better age group. It's about understanding who is sitting in front of you - and giving them lessons they'll actually remember.
