Teaching B2–C1 Adults Online: 8 Things I've Learned the Hard Way

20 July 2026 · ESL-plans Teacher's Corner

Teaching advanced adults online sounds easy on paper. They already speak English. They know how online meetings work. They're motivated. Well... sometimes. The reality is a little messier.

Over the last 7 years, I've taught online everyone from sleepy engineers joining from their cars (parked, thankfully!) to executives connecting from airport lounges twenty minutes before boarding. I've had students lose internet halfway through a discussion about philosophy, answer my questions while walking their dog, and once finish a lesson from the queue at IKEA. That's adult online teaching. Their lives don't stop because English class starts. Instead of fighting that reality, I've learned to embrace it. Here are eight things that have genuinely made my online lessons smoother - for both me and my students.

1. Be platform-flexible

Everyone says, "Let's use Zoom." Until Zoom decides not to cooperate.

  • Or your student can't install it because of company restrictions.
  • Or they only have Microsoft Teams on their work laptop.
  • Or Google Meet is the only thing their IT department allows.

Don't become emotionally attached to one platform. I keep Zoom, Teams and Google Meet ready to go. If one fails, we switch in minutes instead of cancelling the lesson. Students appreciate teachers who solve problems instead of creating them. Technology should disappear into the background.

2. Assume your student is using a phone

This completely changed the way I design lessons. A huge number of adults don't sit comfortably behind a large monitor with a coffee. They're on trains. In hotel rooms. In cafés. Waiting for a meeting. Using nothing but a phone. If your worksheet only works on a 27-inch monitor, it doesn't really work. Nowadays, whenever I create a lesson, I ask myself one simple question:

"Could someone comfortably use this on a phone?"

That usually means:

  • large fonts
  • clean layouts
  • one task at a time
  • clear visuals
  • plenty of white space

Tiny paragraphs and microscopic vocabulary boxes may look elegant on your computer. On a phone, they're a nightmare. 
It might sound contradictory given the lesson plan designs presented here, but there's a trick: all materials are designed in blocks that can be easily cut out in MS Paint in seconds. Adapt the lessons on the go!

3. Let visuals do some of the teaching

Adults process visual information incredibly quickly. Instead of explaining everything for two minutes, sometimes one good image does the job in five seconds. Whenever possible, I replace long instructions with arrows, icons, colour coding or examples. Students spend less time figuring out what they're supposed to do and more time actually speaking English. That's exactly why many of my lesson plans lean heavily on visuals rather than walls of text. The less cognitive effort students spend navigating the worksheet, the more energy they have for the language itself.

4. Expect the lesson to end unexpectedly

One lesson taught me this forever. Everything was going brilliantly when my student suddenly looked at another screen and said, "Sorry, Alex... emergency meeting. I have to go." Lesson over. No goodbye activity. No homework. Nothing. Since then, I always keep the final few minutes flexible. If we finish normally, great. If life interrupts -and it often does - I already know what's essential to cover first.

Adults have jobs, children, deadlines and unpredictable schedules. Sometimes English simply loses to real life. Don't take it personally.

5. Follow up after every lesson

This is probably the simplest habit I've developed, and one of the most valuable.

Adults are busy. Really busy. By tomorrow morning, they've already attended three meetings, replied to fifty emails and forgotten half the vocabulary from the lesson. A quick follow-up message makes a huge difference.

Maybe it's:

  • today's homework
  • five useful expressions
  • a podcast recommendation
  • a reminder about next week's topic
  • one mistake worth reviewing

Nothing fancy. Just enough to reconnect them with the lesson. Think of it as leaving little breadcrumbs back to English.

6. Be politely persistent

Here's something new teachers often misunderstand. If an adult student forgets a lesson, it usually isn't because they don't care. They're overwhelmed.

I've had students apologise because they remembered the lesson halfway through a business dinner. Others realised they had booked a dentist appointment over our class. Life happens.
Don't be afraid to send reminders.  Confirm lessons. Follow up if someone disappears. Check in after a cancelled class. You're not being annoying. You're helping busy people keep a promise they genuinely wanted to keep. Sometimes teachers worry they'll seem too pushy. In my experience, adults usually appreciate a gentle nudge.

7. Talk about things adults actually care about

By B2 or C1, grammar is rarely the biggest challenge. Keeping someone genuinely interested for an hour is.

That's why I spend far more time choosing the topic than choosing the grammar point.

People happily forget they're "studying English" when they're debating whether AI will replace their jobs, discussing workplace culture or arguing about human behaviour. Lessons like AI Anxiety, The Culture of Overwork or Dealing with Change work well not because they're linguistically revolutionary, but because people actually have something to say. Once the conversation starts flowing, the language usually follows.

8. Relax a little

This might be the most important advice of all. Advanced students don't expect perfection. They expect authenticity. They don't need you to perform like a TV presenter. They want a conversation. A challenge. A laugh.

Sometimes they'll ask questions you can't answer. Sometimes you'll both end up Googling a word. Sometimes the discussion will drift somewhere completely unexpected. Good.

Those moments often become the most memorable part of the lesson. After all these years, I've realised that the best online classes rarely feel like classes. They feel like two curious people exploring an interesting topic together. Everything else - grammar, vocabulary, pronunciation - quietly happens in the background. And honestly, that's my favourite kind of teaching. Or, as I like to think of it... Teaching without really feeling like you're teaching at all.
